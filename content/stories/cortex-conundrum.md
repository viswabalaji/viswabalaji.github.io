Arun woke up to the sound of the popular "morning lights" ringtone. It was the middle of the night. The stars and the moon lay hidden by the clouds. His parched throat yearned for a glass of ice-cold water. Sweeping his hands around in the dark, he reached out to the water bottle kept on the bedside table.

"Turn the bedroom lights on" he spoke to his smart watch on his right wrist. Two yellow light bulbs placed in far corners of the room lit up. He pushed the sheets away and lethargically got out of his bed, having grown accustomed to sleeping late every day. Afraid of staring at the blank ceiling for hours, he had always gone to bed late.

A few small contract jobs here and there helped him live a scrambling life from paycheck to paycheck. The uncertainty of contract renewals was enough to disturb his sleep. The pressures of his lifestyle caused his wife and kids anxiety too. Like everyone who craves stability, Arun was on the lookout for a decent stable job. A silver bullet which would enable his family to leave behind the dilapidated house in the rural town and move back in with him in the city.

The fresh smell of coffee from the kitchen counter caught his attention. His watch notified him his morning coffee was ready. He rushed to the bathroom to brush his teeth. It was an everyday competition he had created to get to the coffee before it turned cold. What better motivation to jumpstart another day of a meaningless existence?

The electric toothbrush churned away at his teeth and pink gums while he stood on the weighing scale. His smart watch indicated his health index values had not changed significantly since the past year he had been away from home. His poor urban diet and stress were steadily chipping away at his body and mental stability. After brushing, he gulped down the coffee along with the daily news briefing displayed on the seventy-inch screen mounted on the wall.

The employment opportunities in the country were projected to increase rapidly over the next few months, after hitting an all-time low for the past half decade. The perfected invention of the Cortex chip by Noel Lark had taken the world by storm. Approval of this product by the Doctor's Association had increased its appeal and appeased safety concerns. This news brought a sigh of relief to the millions of people who had lost their jobs to automatons. The synergy of man and machine would mean that individuals would either have their job back or would be in a better state of mind to imbibe more information to enter a new field with ease. At least this is what Noel Lark promised when he shared his unique invention with the world. His reassuring smile, graceful pose, and plainspoken attitude did not show any signs of corporate malfeasance or sinister motives. The masses accepted him as their savior.

Arun was among the individuals who were enthused by this stunning discovery. It seemed like he could revert to his cherished life of being a chartered accountant. Automatons, though aware of a multitude of tax rules and advanced mathematics, still lacked human creativity to navigate the complex tactics people use to exploit loopholes. A smart human aid was necessary. Though not at the top of his field, Arun believed that the Cortex chip and his internal drive were sufficient to get himself reinstated. He had signed himself up to be one of the first three million recipients to undergo a minor operation to get the Cortex chip inserted into the cerebrum and spinal cord. One wafer-thin chip to help with thinking and mental activity and a smaller chip to help with physical activity in the spinal cord. The surgery was scheduled for late afternoon today. Arun had planned to take an early morning flight to the city in the far south of the country.

His conservative-minded acquaintances had warned him against taking part in the Cortex trials due to privacy concerns. Conspiracy theory news outlets had a field day for the past two weeks since the launch of the product. To Arun, this was an opportunity to move ahead with the world, a survival of the fittest strategy. To him, the Cortex chip was no different from his smart watch.

"It is not a brain control technology; we are nowhere near that. Besides, we have specialized machines that can be produced and programmed more easily than humans for tasks, so taking over control of the brain is not of any use to us at all. We are not a marketing & advertising company; we are the next generation intelligent machine company. You can turn off the Cortex chip at your own liking. Let me iterate this once more. You control the chip, you give it instructions, no info ever leaves the chip unless you allow it to. I have open sourced the device and its contents to the world. You can look for yourself to see if we even collect a shred of your data without your consent. This product is only going to aid your brain, it is like attaching multiple processors to make your computer run faster."

Noel Lark's words made complete sense to Arun. "After all, this is helping me get my lost job back. I can trade off a bit of privacy for the freedom to live with my family. Lark is already a 78-year-old trillionaire thanks to his other ventures, he certainly isn't motivated to scam us now", he reasoned.

His wife had questioned his decision initially, but she too later succumbed under financial pressure. She had given birth to two kids; it was her duty to ensure that she did everything possible to grant them a good education and life in this ever-increasing world of automation. Arun recalled the first time he met his wife on a dating platform years ago. She had teased him for wearing a pair of brightly colored socks. As a practical joke, she had gifted him a monthly designer socks subscription.

A bright smile lit up his face, even today, as he wore the colored socks and adjusted his winter coat. His smart watch had predicted a light snowfall. His taxi, automatically ordered based on his flight time, arrived. He buckled into the driver's seat, set the destination, and relaxed back into his seat as his travel music playlist started to play in the background.

He fondly remembered the times when vehicle traffic was a massive problem in urban areas. Since the boon of autonomous driving had occurred, congestion had become an issue of the past. The warm orange hue of the streetside lamps faded into each other. The moonlight slowly made its way through the clouds. His head was clear now.

## After thoughts

"Democratizing intelligence. If everyone were to think through situations, see through fake news, build opinions on recognized facts, understand that optimal solutions to certain problems require a personal sacrifice, then wouldn't we vote wisely, wouldn't we value everyone's logic, opinion and treat them equally, wouldn't the world be a better place?"

"OR would we use the newfound brain power to create even smarter machines to replace us completely? Would we lose individuality if rationality is global? If yes, is it wrong to trade off individuality for rationality? Maybe individual creativity is just a cover for randomness, maybe it is best to have this randomness to reach a global optimal solution than just having an overall rational approach?"

Questions abound as we take on the reins of time to search for the meaning of existence. If life has meaning, then pursuit of it is wherein lies the meaning. If life has no meaning, then the pursuit of showing meaninglessness spins up meaning. Hence, to find meaning is a burden on the brain. Even if we are successful in doing so, we will never know with certainty if there is absolute meaning or meaninglessness. Like a dog trying to catch its tail our mind spins, where "trying to catch the tail" is the tail itself realized, our mind wins. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle applied to finding meaning.
