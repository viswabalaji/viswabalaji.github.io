Badri stood still staring at his reflection in the mirror. He wished the mirror were a portal into an alternate reality. He could no longer look at his half-burned face which pained even when a single muscle moved. To smile or to frown created a chilling pain to the bone. Antibiotic cream and makeup covered up the third-degree burns. Self-pity and endorphins helped suppress the incident from his mind. The jail warden ordered him to finish quickly and get back to his cell. He had to wake up early for court tomorrow.

He could not control his mind; pyrophilia had been his first memory and his most recent too. His love of fire, the emotion of watching material go down in flames and turn into ash, was the fastest way of showing anyone that all life is a cycle. Born from dirt are we, burned to dirt shall we be. He was unable to grapple with why others around him were not like him. "How can my neighbors not like to see their belongings burn? What is the point of saving money to buy materials and not burn them? What else could be driving them otherwise? They probably have broken minds."

Right from his childhood, he had been proud of burning down all types of objects. His tricycle, his schoolyard bushes, his younger brother's hair, his family heirloom portrait had all faced his wrath. He was envious of his younger brother who easily earned praise for studying, playing sports, listening to music and his other natural traits. Whilst he was always scorned, rejected, and beaten for following his authentic self.

He never wanted to hurt anyone; he was just a man who liked playing with fire. When he was sixteen, he had unfortunately set a dog kennel on fire, assuming the dogs were outside, but two small puppies were trapped inside and burned alive. Badri felt sorry for them, yet he could not stop himself from his indulgences. He was sent to the state mental hospital for treatment, but the doctors could not find any psychological or traumatic issues explaining his non-standard behavior. He was sent back home with medications. But these relaxing pills were of no avail to him. Badri was doing all this with forethought and a calm mind. He was not an agitated psycho.

Two days earlier, his family had planned a small party for his 21st birthday. He had no friends; his family was the only social interaction he ever had since no one else was able to understand his proclivities. It was that fateful night he had planned to burn down the neighbor's house.

Mr. Pollard and his family had lived in the blue condo next door for the past 15 years. His back had given out from his years of service as a mechanic and placed him in a wheelchair. His wife had passed away a few years ago. Only his 15-year-old twin daughters lived with him. They had posted about an upcoming trip to the nearby coastal city on social media.

Badri loaded the cans of diesel he had stowed in the garage onto the wheelbarrow. He had used up all his earnings of the past year to purchase fuel and accelerants. He watched the twins help their father into the car, load their bags, and drive into the sunset. The final rays of the sun shone on his smiling face. He scaled the fence swiftly and his feet landed on the soft, green, well-maintained yard. In no time, the external walls of the house were reeking of diesel. He set the house on fire and ran into the front yard, waiting for the grand spectacle to take place.

It was then he noticed the open garage door. Mr. Pollard had forgotten his medication. The car had returned to the home. Badri was stunned. He could not move a muscle. He was torn between informing his neighbors about the fire or staying back to enjoy it. In no time, the house was ablaze. Fire raged on from bedroom to bedroom, consuming everything in its path. Badri had cut down a tree on the access road an hour ago to prevent emergency vehicles from reaching there on time. There was nothing left to do now. The screams of the young girls, the wailing cry of Mr. Pollard, and the slow burning sound of wood were the only sounds that could be heard in the silent remote neighborhood.

Badri's family, who had just settled into bed after the birthday event, rushed outside on hearing the noises. The sight of Badri standing in front of the burning house terrified them. Truly this was not someone human, it was someone insidious.

Emergency response vehicles arrived after an hour. Firemen rushed inside with water hoses but to no avail. A home no longer stood, gratifying ruins taking its place. Mr. Pollard's wheelchair was rolled out with bodily appendages and a pile of ash. One of the daughters' gold bracelets hung from the handle of the wheelchair. Seeing the bodies and the raging fire subside, Badri attempted to run towards the flaming ruins but officers restricted him before he was engulfed by the flames. Half of his face burned off in the process.

The courtroom thronged with crowds of representatives from various institutions and media mobs armed with cameras. Protests raged outside: half demanding the death penalty, half demanding life imprisonment. "What good is the death penalty? It is not going to deter future arsonists!" they exclaimed.

"It is not a lesson for the great sinner, but a balancing act of morality, avenging the death of the Pollard family. So that our hearts can be convinced that justice has prevailed," claimed the rest. "Why an instant death then? The burden of guilt is sure to strap him down for the rest of his lifetime in jail," clamored the pacifists. "To hell with guilt. For that, he must recognize his mistake, but a freak cannot do that. He thinks what he did is right, he ain't feeling guilty! He would be a burden on the state forever then," the protestors clamored back.

## After thoughts

Norms of society are dictated by the majority of a particular dominion. Is there absolute truth and morals? Probability indicates that morals are relative and adapt to the environment. Anyone behaving outside a defined and accepted moral boundary is punished, it is the enforcement that makes it all powerful.

Can humans justify the death penalty? We can execute people society deems immoral and corrupt beyond a certain proof of doubt, but establishing this line is where the problem lies. Can you live a peaceful life if murderers roam free? Why should lives be wasted in maintaining facilities for the deemed outcasts?

Is there any possibility of rehabilitation for individuals who got into this situation in the first place with their machinations? If fear of the death penalty cannot stop them, will fear of life imprisonment? Society evolves to weed out the outliers in favor of stability and sustainability, but it is these positive or negative outliers that have several times pushed the defined boundaries of society outside our known world into the unknown realms. Fantasy, creativity, imagination dance along the boundaries of reality and insanity. Controlled in one. Uncontrolled in the other.
