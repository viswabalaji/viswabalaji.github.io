I finished my undergraduate degree in Mathematics, Computer Science, and Statistics from Purdue University in 2.5 years. It was an exhilarating experience trying to manage social, academic, and personal hobby schedules. Days shortened and meshed together with back-to-back lectures. Nights lengthened into intensive hours of lively debate and debugging sessions. I chose to pursue my undergrad in the US instead of India after careful consideration. Program flexibility was one of the core reasons.

## Why I Chose to Graduate Early

### Cost of Attendance

Purdue University charged a flat fee every semester, so you could take 12 credits or 30 credits worth of courses for the same tuition. A grand total of 120 credits is the graduation requirement. Several classes also have a test-out option for reduced fees that are easier to study for and obtain credits if you have the necessary prerequisite knowledge and experience. For an international student, a semester usually costs around $20K, so graduating earlier leads to significantly less student debt. In this case, 50% less student debt. Interning in the summer months helped towards tuition as well.

### Pushing Your Boundaries

I did not come into university with my mind set on finishing the program early. I started my first semester like any other student with 15 credits. I realized I had a lot of free time on my hands, pursued cooking as a hobby, and started working part-time at the dining court to have a practical experience outlet for my newfound skills. It was a stressful job for minimum wage. I had also joined a community service organization in an effort to form new social connections with fellow volunteers and engage meaningfully in the community. Yet I was not satisfied. I knew I could do more. I was bored.

I decided to add 3 credits in my next semester to ensure I had enough coursework to keep me mentally occupied and excited. The next semester I took 18, then 21 after that, 24, and then 27 in my final semester.

### Control of Time

A cliche argument against graduating early is that one wants to soak in the 4-year college experience as it is the best time of one's life. If one has arbitrarily assumed that the best experiences in life are only in college, then what are they looking forward to in the coming years? Do they not expect to form meaningful connections in their late 20s? Seems like a low standard to set. Long-term time gain is ignored here.

Finishing the program in 2.5 years gifted me the remaining 1.5 years to meet more new people, explore places with more freedom, gain practical work experience, and get a head start in settling down with financial stability. Also, it was a great 1.5 years with no exam or homework stress.

### Stronger Social Connections

When managing multiple classes and projects, you also learn to manage your social life well. I would never have been able to complete all my courses without help from my friends and classmates. I was unable to attend office hours for each class or keep up with important notifications and modifications for every single class. A diligent group of friends is required to triage information.

This led to greater social connections since the quality of a friendship does not only depend on length but on the intensity of the give-and-take interaction. High-energy, high-powered situations weathered through with peers leads to overall better bonding. A clear hidden advantage is exposure to peer groups from different batches, whose experiential advice and their own well-established cliques quickly enrich your social network as well.

### Brain Inferencing Rate

This is a unique psychological perspective that I came up with from personal experience, which may or may not be applicable to you. I call it the inferencing rate upper and lower bound. If you take on too many items on your plate at a time and cross your brain's upper bound, you perform poorly in all tasks due to the cascading negative effect of performing poorly in one due to lack of time. On the other hand, if you take on too little work below your lower bound inference rate, then your mind wanders and ponders about other items, causing distraction which leads to poor performance again.

There is a sweet spot between the upper and lower bound where, if you have enough tasks that support this inferencing rate, the brain is able to finish tasks in a round-robin manner. Solving each task positively reinforces solving other tasks, leading to good stable performance. This bound certainly differs across students, but one has to be willing to explore outside the bounds to get to know their limits. This explorative nature is all that matters.

## Conclusion

I realize that not everyone might want to fast-track their education. For instance, academia-oriented students have a greater advantage staying longer to get valuable research experience under their belt. If industry work experience is where your heart is at, then it is best to use the university as a springboard to jump and land into your desired domain.

With the rise of digital learning and online certifications, it is clear that universities no longer have a monopoly. Thus, in order to get to the end destination in a cost and time-optimized manner, fast-track is a great option to consider.
